# Discussant decks

A discussion is its own genre with two jobs (Dallas Fed): help the audience
understand the paper, and help the author improve it. Every slide serves one
of the two. Sources: Dallas Fed "Tips on Being a Good Discussant", ASHEcon
"How to Be a Good Discussant".

## Time and size discipline

First determine what the clock means. An organizer's “10-minute discussion”
normally states the discussant's speaking allocation; do not reserve another
question share from it. If the user instead gives a total session owned by the
discussant, plan 75–80% for prepared remarks and reserve 20–25% for questions,
as in any other presentation. Build the frame count from the strength and
depth of the comments rather than a quota. Run slightly *under* the speaking
allocation. Discussant decks are normally static so the room can inspect the
paper and proposed revision without waiting for decorative reveals.

## An adaptable discussant path

The roles below are a useful starting path, not a mandatory frame order or
count. A clearly presented paper may need only one summary sentence; a theory
paper may replace Design with Model; one consequential comment may deserve
more time than three modest ones. Keep the smallest sequence that lets the
audience understand the authors' contribution and the proposed improvement.

1. **Title** — the *paper's* title as `\title`; then "by [authors]"; then
   "Discussion by [name] (affiliation)". Disclaimer footnote if the
   discussant's employer requires one.
2. **One-slide summary** — the paper compressed into `\RunIn` blocks:
   **Question:** / **Design:** (or **Model:**) / **Findings:** — one line
   each. Add a small flow diagram only when the relational structure cannot be
   stated as clearly in those lines; the source-first figure gate still
   applies. Calibrate to the
   room: if the speaker just explained the paper clearly, compress to two
   lines or drop (ASHEcon: don't re-summarize); keep it if the talk was
   confusing or the audience is mixed (Dallas Fed).
3. **What the paper does well** — genuine and specific support
   ("things I enjoyed"), plus where it sits in the literature if that adds
   orientation. Generous but never empty praise.
4. **Comment slides (the core)** — one frame per consequential comment:
   - The frame title *is* the comment, as a one-line actionable assertion
     or question ("The placebo tests the wrong threat", "Can selection on
     growth potential explain the pre-trend pattern?") — never "Comment 1".
   - Body: one line restating what the paper does (highlighted), then the
     concern as analysis — with the discussant's own back-of-envelope
     calculation, alternative specification, or counter-example where
     possible.
   - Each comment **ends in a concrete `\RunIn{Suggestion:}` line**: a
     specific robustness check, a data source, a reframing — something the
     author can do Monday morning. Do not use an arrow as the bullet marker.
5. **Summary, when useful** — the paper's takeaway restated fairly, then the
   main comments as one-liners. End positive: this is a paper worth improving.
6. **"Other comments"** (optional, `[noframenumbering]`, often not
   presented) — the grab-bag of smaller points: minor specification
   suggestions, presentation nits, related literature. Typos and citation
   fixes go to the author *privately*, never on screen.

## Tone rules

- **Be 10% kinder than feels natural** (ASHEcon). The author is often
  junior, and the room is judging your judgment too.
- Comments are analysis, not attack: substantiate every criticism; lead
  with the fix, not the flaw; frame as questions where genuine.
- Big-picture over minutiae: one or two points that could change the paper
  beat ten that polish it (Dallas Fed).
- Never apologize for being an imperfect fit for the paper; never perform
  superiority; never discuss a different paper than the one written
  ("the paper I would have written" is not a comment).
- Share the slides with the author afterward — they double as a written
  mini-referee-report, which is exactly how to prepare (read once for
  impression, list questions, re-read answering them, check the key
  table/equation, then outline).

## The spoken layer

A discussion benefits from a script more than any other genre — the time
budget is unforgiving. See `references/speaker-script.md` §Genre variants:
generous open, summary in the authors' favor, hedged titled comments each
landing on a concrete suggestion, generous close.

## Number provenance (applies with extra force)

Every number quoted from the paper carries its table/page tag in a comment.
The discussant's own calculations are labeled as such on the slide
("back-of-envelope: 0.032 × 0.20 SD ≈ 0.6%") — the audience must never
mistake the discussant's arithmetic for the paper's.

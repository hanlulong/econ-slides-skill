% Replace every bracketed prompt with source-faithful language.  The prose
% explains the intellectual job of each block; it is not a word-count target.
\scriptopening{about 0.4 min}{%
  Good morning. I am \PresenterName, and today I will present
  \emph{\PaperTitle}, joint work with \CoauthorsSpoken, here at \VenueShort.
  \TitleDisclaimer\ The paper asks [state the Key question in one sentence].
  The answer turns on [name the economic tension], and I will show why that
  force matters before stating the formal result.
  \Cue{Greeting; paper and coauthors; Key question; economic tension.}
}

% Keep this title synchronized with theory-talk.tex. If the question is too
% long for a frame title, change both files to the literal {Key question}
% fallback and state \KeyQuestion in the body and spoken opening.
\scriptframe{\KeyQuestion}{about 0.8 min}{%
  The paper starts from a simple tension. [State the behavior, allocation, or
  policy margin in concrete terms.] One force pushes the relevant choice in
  one direction: [explain the first force]. A second force pushes back:
  [explain the opposing force]. Existing results do not settle which force
  dominates because [name the missing equilibrium margin or restriction].

  The question is therefore [repeat the Key question in the same language as
  the deck]. The object we need is [name the equilibrium, policy, or welfare
  object]. This formulation matters because it distinguishes a private
  response from the equilibrium response the paper studies. The next block
  gives the answer before we build the model that supports it.
  \Cue{The paper starts from a simple tension.}
}

% Select and order the paper's real formal payoffs rather than forcing a fixed
% result/mechanism/application count.  Possibilities include tractability,
% decomposition, representation, comparative statics, welfare, and policy.
\scriptframe{This paper}{about 0.8 min}{%
  Here is what we do and what we find. \ResultHeadlineSpoken. In economic terms, [say who
  changes which choice and in response to what]. The mechanism is [state the
  force in one clause]. It works because [give the causal logic in words,
  without notation].

  The paper also shows \ResultSupportSpoken. That comparison matters because
  [explain what it reveals about the mechanism or scope]. Save technical
  conditions for the proposition or environment block, where the audience can
  understand what they do. The implication is \ResultImplicationSpoken.
  \Cue{Here is what we do and what we find.}
}

\scriptframe{Roadmap}{about 0.3 min}{%
  I will make three stops. I will set out the environment and the economic
  tension, isolate the mechanism behind the main proposition, and then show
  the comparative static or application that gives the result its meaning.
  \Cue{I will make three stops.}
}

\scriptframe{Environment}{about 0.8 min}{%
  The environment has [name the agents] and [name the payoff-relevant state].
  First, [describe the initial move and what is known]. Then [describe the
  next move, information, and any uncertainty]. Each agent chooses [action]
  subject to [resource, technology, or incentive constraint].

  The solution concept is [name and explain the equilibrium or optimum]. In
  ordinary language, it requires [state what beliefs, prices, strategies, or
  allocations must be mutually consistent]. The key assumption is
  [state it precisely]. Economically, that assumption does one job: [explain
  the margin it disciplines]. Everything else can remain in the appendix
  unless it changes how the audience should read the proposition.
  \Cue{The environment has [name the agents] and [name the payoff-relevant state].}
}

% This is the singular/default mechanism block.  Duplicate it for competing
% forces, keep the same object/current-decision/equilibrium-effect/sign order,
% and add a synthesis block before the proposition.  Do not split a compact
% complementary chain merely for symmetry.
\scriptframe{Mechanism}{about 0.8 min}{%
  Now isolate the mechanism. The condition on the screen compares [economic
  gain] with [economic cost or wedge]. I will not read the notation. The first
  term says [give its economic meaning]; the second captures [give its
  economic meaning].

  [For a higher-order result, describe two future states, hold today's state
  and the relevant mean fixed, and widen the spread. Say whose current choice
  changes and why the expected payoff, price, or return is nonlinear.]

  Holding equilibrium objects fixed, [primitive] changes the agent's choice
  through [direct force]. But other agents or prices respond as well. That
  response changes [the relevant payoff, constraint, or strategic incentive].
  Under the key assumption, the feedback is [stronger or weaker]
  than the direct force, so the net effect has the sign stated on the next
  frame. The useful intuition is [give a short concrete analogy or limiting
  case supported by the model].
  \Cue{Now isolate the mechanism.}
}

\scriptframe{Main proposition}{about 0.9 min}{%
  The proposition says \ResultHeadlineSpoken. That is the statement in words.
  The formal line makes two points precise: [name the object being compared]
  and [name the condition under which the sign or ordering holds]. Define any
  symbol the audience has not already heard by its economic meaning.

  Why is the proposition true? Start from [the agent's best response or value
  comparison]. The mechanism changes [the first key margin]. Equilibrium then
  changes [the feedback margin]. The key condition lets us order
  those two changes, which delivers the result. The full proof is in the
  appendix. What matters here is the economic step that signs the proposition,
  not the algebra used to verify regularity.
  \Cue{The proposition says \ResultHeadlineSpoken.}
}

\scriptframe{Economic interpretation}{about 0.7 min}{%
  Now let me show where the proposition matters. \ResultSupportSpoken. [Explain the
  comparative static, welfare result, or application without introducing a
  new model.] The result changes [agent]'s choice because [mechanism].
  % Add one short contrast only when the room is likely to confuse the object;
  % do not force a defensive ``not X'' sentence into every script.

  The economically relevant comparison is [state the benchmark or special
  case]. Relative to that benchmark, [describe the direction and, when the
  paper supplies one, the scale]. This tells us [state what the result changes
  for behavior, welfare, or policy]. That is why the formal proposition matters
  outside the proof.
  % Add one spoken boundary sentence here only if omitting it would materially
  % misstate this interpretation; do not keep a generic scope prompt.
  \Cue{Now let me show where the proposition matters.}
}

\begin{samepage}
\scriptframe{Conclusion}{about 0.7 min}{%
  Let me close with the answer. \ResultHeadlineSpoken. The result follows from
  [name the mechanism in one sentence], and \ResultSupportSpoken{} shows where
  that force is strongest or most useful. Recall the scope condition only if
  leaving it out would make the conclusion false.

  The broader implication is \ResultImplicationSpoken. The paper's
  contribution is to show how [primitive or friction] changes [equilibrium or
  welfare object] once [feedback or strategic response] is allowed to operate.
  End on that economic contribution. Do not reopen the proof or introduce a
  new extension.
  \Cue{Let me close with the answer.}
}
\end{samepage}

\ScriptAppendix

\scriptframe{Proof logic}{if asked}{%
  Begin with [best response, envelope condition, or value comparison]. The
  first step establishes [intermediate result]. The second step imposes
  equilibrium consistency, which changes [key feedback margin]. The
  key condition then signs that change. The remaining steps verify
  existence, regularity, or boundary cases; they do not create the economic
  result. Point to the exact lemma or proposition in the paper if more detail
  is requested.
}

\scriptframe{Alternative assumption}{if asked}{%
  The baseline assumption is [state it], and it controls [economic margin].
  Under [weaker or different assumption], the mechanism still changes
  [object], so [part of the result] survives. What may change is [sign, scope,
  uniqueness, or comparative-static strength]. This separates a convenient
  sufficient condition from the economic force itself and makes clear which
  conclusion is robust to the alternative.
}

\scriptframe{Extension or benchmark}{if asked}{%
  The extension adds [agent, state, friction, or instrument]. Relative to the
  benchmark, it changes [incentive or equilibrium feedback]. The original
  mechanism is preserved when [condition], but it can weaken or reverse when
  [condition]. The point of the extension is therefore not extra generality
  by itself. It shows which part of the main result comes from the core force
  and which part depends on the baseline environment.
}

\section*{Standing Q\&A stance}
Hear the full question and count to three. State the economic answer first,
then add notation only if it makes the answer more precise. Concede a genuine
scope condition directly and separate the mechanism from convenient proof
assumptions.
